Project Overview

Brief Description

Getting Ahead was my second of three university capstone projects. It's my first multiplayer project, though luckily I had spent the previous summer break developing my Kickstarter Package which included a local multiplayer input system. The primary gameplay loop consists of player spawning in one room, and the players must work together to defeat all the enemies in a room before they are able to move on to the next room. Whenever a player takes too much damage and dies, they will be decapitated but aren't out yet, instead if there is an available body in the room they can 'recapitate' that body, gaining the attacks and statistics associated with the body. Players can also leave their current body whenever they wish if there is a more suitable body available for them, or if they wish to mess with their friends and steal a body from them.

Players will be assigned one of two classes randomly at the start of each game, either the melee Knight / Squire or the ranged Wizard. Enemy classes will be room dependent, with each room having some amount of points and will spawn enemies until it runs out of points. Enemy classes include the Goblin, Salamander, Golem, and Zombie. There is friendly fire between both enemies and players, encouraging players to think about how they might make enemies attack each other, while also including the opprotunity for them to attack each other, creating a very playful experience.

Contributions and Responsibilities

This was the first large scale project on which I was the tech team lead. This meant that I was responsible for ensuring that the game could be completed by managing and reworking the design to fit an appropriate scope throughout the project. I was also responsible for assigning issues to each member of my team at the start of each development sprint.

Challenges Faced

  1. As this was my first leadership role, I had to learn how to handle assignments of issues. Initially, I took the route of assigning an equal ammount of work to each person for each sprint, often based on the amount of work I would be doing. I quickly realized that this meant I was overworking my teammates, as they didn't have the available time that I had, and so I needed a solution.
  2. I had to develop a whole system for how the decapitation and recapitation would function. This was my most complex game mechanic that I had designed at the time, so I made sure to think about it deeply before beginning the process. Ultimately, this made the system far easier to produce, though with one issue that I only realized far later in development. On some cases when a player would die, the decapitation would be fail somewhere, causing the body to keep moving, and the head be unable to recapitate.

Lessons Learned

  1. I had taken the route of looking at each member on my team individually. I would ask them what they felt comfortable working on so that I could feel confident they knew how to do it and I'd also consider how long the task would likely take to complete and compare with the time per week they were able to commit to the project. This ultimately balanced out development a lot better. I had a lot more time available to work on the project and so I was giving myself the bigger tasks in development, meanwhile the other developers would get smaller tasks to ensure that they could still complete it within a single sprint.
  2. To develop the capitation system, I had gotten out my whiteboard and written out the step for each variation of capitations, from enemy decapitations, player decapitations, and player recapitations. That was the key that made developing this system so much easier. However, the bug came from a remnant in the capitation system from before I had developed the enemy AI. Once the AI was implemented, it revealed a bug due to player registration in the capitation controller. Ultimately, it was simple to solve as it was just legacy code from before I implemented AI for enemies.
